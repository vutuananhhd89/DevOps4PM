# Deep Dive Digest – March 1, 2026

> **Topic**: Three LeadDev 2025 Reports — The Full State of Engineering Leadership, Team Performance, and AI Impact
> **Deep Dive Sources**:
>
> 1. **LeadDev Engineering Leadership Report 2025** — 617 engineering leaders (March 2025)
> 2. **Engineering Team Performance Report 2025** — 500 engineering leaders (July–August 2025, with O'Reilly)
> 3. **AI Impact Report 2025** — 883 engineering leaders (May–June 2025, with DX)
> **Context**: Three simultaneous primary-source reports from LeadDev create an unusually rich dataset. Together they document engineering leadership under pressure: org restructuring hitting managers hard, measurement frameworks in crisis, and AI delivering far less than promised — while everyone keeps spending anyway.

## Part 1: Engineering Leadership Report 2025

**Source**: [LeadDev Engineering Leadership Report 2025](https://leaddev.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/LeadDev-Engineering-Leadership-Report-2025.pdf)
**Sample**: 617 engineering leaders · Survey: March 14–27, 2025
**Editor-in-Chief**: Scott Carey · **Locations**: USA 34%, Europe 20%, UK 20%, Germany 10%

---

### 📊 The Five Headline Numbers

| Finding | Stat |
|---------|------|
| Don't believe AI significantly boosted team productivity | **60%** |
| Worry there are fewer jobs available (up from 43% in 2024) | **50%** |
| Believe their team is less motivated than 12 months ago | **40%** |
| Worried about an upcoming recession | **65%** |
| Organizations that have eliminated DEI programs | **5%** |

> **One sentence**: Engineering leaders in early 2025 were worried, stretched thin, managing flatter structures, and skeptical of AI — and the macro mood was anxious.

---

### 🏢 Section A: How Organizations Are Changing

**The manager cull is real, and it's damaging:**

| What Happened | Stat |
|--------------|------|
| Organizations cutting managerial staffing (vs. 2024) | **+28% increase** |
| Line managers most impacted by cuts | **67%** |
| Middle management impacted | **56%** |
| Upper management impacted | **18%** |

- Hiring freezes actually *decreased* (27% vs. 41% in 2024) — the freezes are over, but now they're cutting layers instead.
- Overall staffing *increased* for **43%** of companies — so the org is growing, but the management layer is being removed.
- **79%** say finding suitable candidates in time remains the #1 hiring challenge.

> **The "Manager Squeeze" Pattern**: Orgs are adding headcount while removing management. The result: individual contributors have more autonomy, but who is mentoring, prioritizing, and unblocking? The data on burnout answers this.

---

### 🔥 Section B: Being an Engineering Leader in 2025

**Scope expansion without support:**

| What Changed for Leaders | Stat |
|--------------------------|------|
| Leaders whose scope of responsibility increased | **65%** |
| Leaders managing more direct reports | **40%** |
| Leaders working more hours than last year | **38%** |
| Leaders facing **critical burnout levels** | **22%** |
| Burnout rate in orgs that *cut* managers | **45%** |

**Where time shifted:**

| Time Increased On | Δ |
|-------------------|---|
| Communicating with stakeholders | **+58%** |
| Strategic planning | **+48%** |
| Regular 1:1s | **+38%** |

| Time Decreased On | Δ |
|-------------------|---|
| Recruiting | **-21%** (largest decrease) |

> **The Trap**: As orgs flatten, surviving managers absorb more direct reports and stakeholder communication. The work that used to be distributed across a management layer now concentrates on fewer people. 22% are at critical burnout — and in orgs that cut managers, nearly half are.

**Motivation collapse in restructured orgs:**

| Organization Type | Team Motivation Drop |
|-------------------|--------------------|
| Orgs that **cut** managers | **56% saw team motivation drop** |
| Orgs that **added** managers | **27% saw motivation drop** |

> The org decision to cut management layers has a near-direct causal link to team motivation. The data is sharp: flatten aggressively and lose motivation. This is not abstract.

---

### 🤖 Section C: AI Impact on Leadership (March 2025 Snapshot)

| AI Productivity Finding | Stat |
|------------------------|------|
| Report AI had **no impact** | **19%** |
| Report AI caused a **decline** | **2%** |
| Report only "slight" improvement (1–10%) | **39%** |
| Consider AI a "game-changer" (>30% improvement) | **Only 6%** |

- **53%** of companies shipped an AI-powered feature (66% in companies with 1,000+ engineers)
- AI coding tools remain the top investment priority for **58%** of organizations
- Quality of output is the #1 AI concern for **78%** — fears over data privacy (44%) and ethics (20%) actually *fell* since 2024

> **The 6% Reality**: Only 6% of engineering leaders see AI as transformative. But 58% are still making it their top investment priority. That is a massive gap between return and commitment.

## Part 2: AI Impact Report 2025

**Source**: [LeadDev AI Impact Report 2025](https://leaddev.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/THE-AI-IMPACT-REPORT-2025-download__LDMO__.pdf)
**Sample**: 883 engineering leaders · Survey: May 30–June 21, 2025
**Partners**: DX (developer experience research firm)

---

### 📊 The Five Headline Numbers

| Finding | Stat |
|---------|------|
| Cite lack of metrics on AI impact as key challenge | **60%** |
| Prioritizing AI for internal engineering processes | **85%** |
| Feel more productive using AI coding tools (personal) | **59%** |
| Expect hiring of junior engineers to decrease long-term | **54%** |
| Have no measures of AI tool impact at all | **~82%** |

---

### 🛠️ Section 1: AI Tooling and Investment

**Adoption spread (not depth):**

| Adoption Stage | % |
|---------------|---|
| Widely adopted across many teams | 31% |
| Selectively adopted for specific use cases | 35% |
| Pilot/POC stage | 20% |
| Exploratory stage | 13% |
| No plans to use AI | 2% |

> **66% have AI in production** — but "in production" ranges from one team using Copilot to org-wide rollout. The data doesn't distinguish depth.

**Who's winning the tool market:**

| Tool | Market Share |
|------|-------------|
| **Cursor** | **45%** |
| GitHub Copilot | 37% |
| OpenAI (ChatGPT/Codex) | 27% |
| Google Gemini | 19% |
| Claude | 16% |

> **Cursor at 45%** is the most striking number in this section — it outranks Copilot among engineering leaders. This reflects the agent-first shift from autocomplete to full-context coding environments.

**Where the money goes:**

| Investment Focus | % |
|-----------------|---|
| Internal engineering processes | **85%** |
| Product features for customers | 63% |
| Staff training | 33% |

**Spending scale (500+ engineer orgs)**: 71% plan to spend **over $100,000 on AI tools** in the next year.

---

### ⚙️ Section 2: Impact on Engineering Tasks

**What AI is actually doing:**

| Task AI Is Most Used For | % |
|--------------------------|---|
| Automated code generation | 48% |
| Summarizing meetings/documentation | 39% |
| Writing documentation/emails | 36% |
| Research and learning | 32% |

**Productivity reality:**

| Outcome | % |
|---------|---|
| Reported increased productivity | **59%** |
| Reported decreased productivity | **10%** |
| Reported >25% productivity boost | **12%** |

- **Technical debt**: 41% saw no significant impact, 23% saw a reduction, **13% reported an increase** in tech debt from AI adoption

> **The 13% Tech Debt Increase**: This is the finding that gets buried. Nearly 1 in 7 orgs report AI *increased* their technical debt burden. Rushing AI-generated code into production without adequate review creates debt faster than teams can service it.

---

### 👥 Section 3: Impact on Hiring and Junior Engineers

This section directly extends and sharpens the Feb 27 findings:

| Finding | Stat |
|---------|------|
| Expect fewer junior engineer hires long-term | **54%** |
| Expect onboarding practices to change | **43%** |
| Believe learning new skills will become harder | **38%** |
| Say AI reduced direct mentoring from seniors | **38%** |

```
THE JUNIOR PIPELINE PARADOX:

AI automates:           AI is reducing:
  Bug fixes        →     The reason to hire juniors
  Small features   →     The tasks that build understanding
  Writing tests    →     The work that creates mentoring moments

Result: 54% expect fewer junior hires
        38% say mentoring has already declined
        Future senior pipeline: unclear
```

---

### 🧠 Section 4: Skills in Demand

**What human skills matter MORE as AI grows:**

| In-Demand Skill (Next 3 Years) | % |
|-------------------------------|---|
| **Critical thinking** | **43%** |
| Architectural design | 34% |
| Domain expertise | 28% |
| Communication skills | 27% |

**What engineers are personally focusing on:**

- Managing AI agents: **60%**
- Prompt engineering: **53%**

> **The signal**: Critical thinking (43%) beats prompt engineering as the #1 skill. The market is saying: AI does the execution, humans need to do the judgment. For PMs, this validates the "PM as orchestrator" thesis — the strategic layer matters more, not less.

---

### 🏗️ Section 5: Team Structure Changes

| Finding | Stat |
|---------|------|
| Reported no significant team structure changes | **52%** |
| Of those who changed: created dedicated AI/ML teams | 52% |
| Of those who changed: reorganized for AI initiatives | 48% |
| Of those who changed: consolidated headcount | 26% |
| Leaders with increased AI initiative scope | **61%** |
| Leaders with increased AI training responsibilities | **49%** |

---

### ⚠️ Section 6: Challenges and Measurement Gap

The single most damning section of the report:

| Challenge | % Citing It |
|-----------|------------|
| **Lack of metrics to evaluate AI impact** | **60%** |
| Security concerns | 49% |
| Response accuracy / hallucinations | 48% |
| Reliability and scalability | 33% |

**Among the 18% who ARE measuring:**

| Metric in Use | % |
|--------------|---|
| Development time per feature | 47% |
| Weekly time saved | 40% |
| Time spent reviewing AI code | 39% |
| Employee sentiment | 37% |

> **82% can't answer "is AI helping?"** — They're investing $100K+ on tools they have no framework to evaluate. And **only 18% have ethics guidelines** for AI use in their organization.

## Part 3: Engineering Team Performance Report 2025

**Source**: [Engineering Team Performance Report 2025](https://leaddev.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/ENGINEERING_TEAM_PERFORMANCE_REPORT-2025.pdf)
**Sample**: 500 engineering leaders · Survey: July 31–August 21, 2025
**Partners**: O'Reilly

---

### 📊 The Five Headline Numbers

| Finding | Stat |
|---------|------|
| Still struggle to choose the right metrics | **53%** (up from 47% in 2024) |
| Explicitly avoid measuring "lines of code" | **65%** |
| Confidence in DORA as the "best" framework (down from 56% in 2024) | **41%** |
| Learning and Development is a priority | **83%** (up from 70% last year) |
| Focusing on upskilling in prompt engineering & managing AI agents | **47%** |

---

### 🎯 Section 1: Goal Setting

| Finding | Stat |
|---------|------|
| Have clear business goals | **89%** |
| Engineering goals are effectively communicated to them | **67%** |
| Board understands the value of engineering | **44%** (down from 51% in 2024) |
| Personally understand how their team contributes to business goals | **82%** |

**Most common goal-communication channels:**

- Regular team meetings: **65%**
- Individual 1:1s: **58%**
- Shared dashboards: **32%**
- Newsletters: **12%**

> **The "Value Gap" is widening.** In 2024, 51% felt the board understood engineering's value. In 2025, that's 44%. As AI spending demands justification, engineering leaders are less confident the board sees what engineering actually delivers.

**Cross-team alignment is the silent bottleneck:**

- **73%** cite alignment with other teams as a major issue
- Teams coordinating *daily* grew to **35%** — more frequent, but more friction

---

### 📏 Section 2: Performance Metrics — The Measurement Crisis

**Why organizations measure (and it's not what you think):**

| Primary Driver | % |
|----------------|---|
| To improve quality | **28%** |
| To identify process bottlenecks | **27%** |
| To increase accountability | 19% |
| To improve velocity | 18% |
| **For personnel decisions (pay/promotions)** | **Only 7%** |

> **Only 7% use metrics for personnel decisions.** This goes against the "surveillance capitalism in engineering" narrative. Most leaders are measuring to improve the system, not manage individuals.

**Metrics actually in use:**

| Metric Used | % |
|-------------|---|
| Number of incidents | **55%** |
| Number of tickets closed | **50%** |
| Deployment frequency | **44%** |
| Cycle time | **38%** |
| Lines of code | **5%** (lowest) |

**Metrics explicitly avoided:**

| Metric Avoided | % |
|----------------|---|
| Lines of code | **65%** |
| Days worked | **35%** |
| Story points | **26%** (gaming concerns) |

> *"We avoid pull requests and lines of code metrics because they encourage quantity over quality and can lead to gaming behaviors... These metrics don't correlate with actual value delivered to users or business outcomes."* — Anonymous CTO respondent

**Framework adoption:**

| Framework | Use Rate | Trend |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| DORA | — | **Down** to 41% confidence (from 56% in 2024) |
| SPACE | 37% | Growing alternative to DORA alone |
| Retrospectives (qualitative) | 64% | Highly used |

> **DORA's decline is significant.** For years, DORA (Deployment Frequency, Lead Time, MTTR, Change Failure Rate) was the de facto standard. Its fall from 56% to 41% confidence in one year reflects: AI-generated code distorting traditional metrics, and leaders recognizing that DORA measures speed-of-delivery but not value-of-delivery.

**Developer experience measurement:**

- **75%** find 1:1 meetings most useful for tracking developer experience
- **64%** use retrospectives for qualitative performance data
- Continuous feedback: **63%**

---

### 📚 Section 3: Learning & Development Surge

**L&D is now a strategic priority, not a benefit:**

| L&D Finding | Stat |
|------------|------|
| L&D is a priority | **83%** (up from 70%) |
| Primary motivation: retain talent and developer satisfaction | **80%** |
| Secondary motivation: boost team performance | **65%** |
| For new strategic business areas | **41%** |

**Top upskilling areas:**

| Upskilling Area | % |
|----------------|---|
| **Prompt engineering and managing AI agents** | **47%** |
| Cloud native technologies | 43% |
| Legacy systems and modernization | 39% |
| Leadership and strategy | 36% |

**L&D barriers:**

- Lack of time: **62%** (primary barrier)
- Need for better incentives (monetary): **31%**

> **AI upskilling is now the #1 L&D priority** for nearly half of engineering orgs. But the #1 barrier is time. This is the L&D paradox: the thing everyone says matters most is the thing nobody has time to do.

## 🔬 Cross-Report Synthesis: The Three Crises

Reading all three reports together reveals three interlocking crises for engineering organizations in 2025:

### Crisis 1: The Measurement Vacuum

```
Team Performance Report: 53% can't choose the right metrics
AI Impact Report:        60% have no metrics for AI impact
Leadership Report:       Board understanding of engineering value ↓ from 51% → 44%

Combined: Organizations are investing more in AI than ever while
          understanding less about whether it's working.
```

**The DORA collapse is the canary.** DORA gave engineering a common language for performance. Its confidence dropped 15 percentage points in one year — suggesting that the existing frameworks weren't built for AI-augmented delivery.

### Crisis 2: The Management Layer Paradox

```
Leadership Report:  28% more orgs cut managers in 2025 vs 2024
                    45% burnout rate in orgs that cut managers
                    56% team motivation drop in manager-cut orgs

AI Impact Report:   61% of remaining leaders gained MORE AI initiative scope
Team Performance:   65% of leaders have increased scope of responsibility
```

**Orgs are simultaneously asking managers to do more (AI strategy, training, stakeholder communication) while removing the management layer.** What gets squeezed out: mentoring, recruiting, and the human connective tissue of engineering orgs.

### Crisis 3: The AI Investment-Return Gap

```
Leadership Report:  Only 6% see AI as a "game-changer"
                    58% still make AI tools their #1 investment priority
AI Impact Report:   12% see >25% productivity boost
                    82% have no metrics to evaluate AI at all
                    13% saw tech debt INCREASE from AI adoption
Team Performance:   47% upskilling in prompt engineering
                    62% say they have no time for L&D
```

**The pattern**: Leaders know AI underdelivers (only 6% see it as transformative), yet continue to invest heavily (100K+ plans, top budget priority). The explanation is probably fear: falling behind peers feels riskier than continuing to invest in something that may not work.

## ✅ What PMs Should Take From All Three Reports

### 1. Own the AI Measurement Problem

The #1 challenge across both the AI Impact and Team Performance reports is measurement. 82% of orgs can't evaluate AI impact. You are uniquely positioned to solve this — PMs define success metrics.

**Concrete actions:**

- [ ] Propose 3–4 leading indicators for AI tool effectiveness (cycle time per feature, defect escape rate, review turnaround time, time-to-first-PR for new hires)
- [ ] Benchmark pre/post AI adoption — even rough data beats no data
- [ ] Frame it as product hypothesis: "We invested in AI tools. Here's what we expected. Here's what we're seeing."

### 2. Advocate for Middle Management

The data is clear: cutting managers doesn't deliver the efficiency savings leadership imagines. It creates burnout (45%), reduces team motivation (56% drop), and removes the mentoring capacity that AI is simultaneously eroding.

**Concrete actions:**

- [ ] Share the burnout and motivation data with leadership before the next restructuring conversation
- [ ] Quantify what managers actually do — the Leadership Report shows their time increasingly goes to stakeholder communication, strategic planning, and 1:1s — not the bureaucracy execs imagine

### 3. Reframe the Junior Developer Question

54% of leaders expect fewer junior hires. But if you also cut managers (who mentor) and AI is reducing hands-on learning opportunities — you're creating a senior engineer shortage in 2–3 years.

**The dependency chain:**

```
Junior hires → Learning tasks → Mentoring from seniors → Senior engineers of the future
(Decreasing) → (AI automating)  → (Reduced per report)  → (Future gap)
```

- [ ] Ask your engineering leaders: "Where will your senior engineers come from in 3 years?"
- [ ] Design AI-era junior onboarding: tasks that pair AI completion with deliberate learning reviews

### 4. Track the Skills Shift

Critical thinking (43%) and architectural design (34%) are what leaders say they need most over the next 3 years. But L&D is dominated by prompt engineering upskilling. These may not contradict each other — but the gap is worth watching.

- [ ] In your team rituals, make space for architectural decision-making discussions that AI can't substitute
- [ ] Use post-mortems and design reviews as "critical thinking reps" that develop the skills employers say they need

### 5. Challenge the DORA Metrics

If your engineering team is using DORA as its performance framework, this is the year to ask whether it still fits. Confidence in DORA fell from 56% to 41% in one year. The SPACE framework (37% adoption) is growing as the richer alternative.

- [ ] Ask: "Does our cycle time metric account for AI-reviewed code taking longer or shorter in review?"
- [ ] Add a qualitative layer: retrospectives (64% use them) and developer sentiment surveys alongside DORA
