## 🗺️ Why This Framework, Why Now

"Product strategy" is one of the most used and least understood terms in product management. Ask 10 PMs what their product strategy is and you'll get:
- A roadmap (that's not strategy)
- A list of OKRs (that's measurement)
- A mission statement (that's aspiration)
- A competitive analysis (that's research)

None of these are strategy.

Chandra Janakiraman's operator's guide fills the gap with a practitioner framework — not academic theory, but a playbook built from running products at Meta and Headspace. His key distinction: there are two types of strategy, and most teams only ever attempt one.

---

## 💡 The Core Thesis

**Strategy is the set of choices about where to play and how to win — not a plan for what to build.**

Janakiraman distinguishes between two types of product strategy:

| Type | Timeline | Purpose | When to Use |
|------|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Small-s strategy** | 8–12 weeks | Solve a specific problem with current resources | Quarterly planning, sprint-level focus |
| **Big-S Strategy** | 5–10 years | Define the product's long-term identity and position | Annual planning, pivots, new product bets |

> *"Most product teams are excellent at small-s strategy — solving the problem in front of them. Very few are good at Big-S Strategy — knowing which problems are worth solving in the first place."*

## ⚙️ The Five-Step Strategy Playbook

### Step 1: Clarify the Strategic Question

Every strategy starts with a question — not a goal, not a metric, but a **question that forces a choice**.

```
Weak: "How do we grow MAU?"
Strong: "Should we grow MAU by expanding to new markets or deepening engagement in existing ones?"
```

The strong version forces a choice. The weak version accepts everything and decides nothing.

**PM Application**: Before your next roadmap review, write the strategic question your team is actually answering. If you can't write it in one sentence, your strategy is unclear.

---

### Step 2: Map the Landscape

Before making choices, understand the terrain:

| Landscape Element | Questions to Answer |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Market dynamics** | Where is demand growing? What's commoditizing? |
| **User segments** | Which users are underserved? Which are over-served? |
| **Competitive position** | Where do we have structural advantages? Disadvantages? |
| **Internal capabilities** | What can our team uniquely deliver? What can't we? |
| **Technology shifts** | What platform or model changes create openings? |

The landscape map is not a list of facts. It's a **narrative** — a story about where the opportunity lies and why.

---

### Step 3: Make the Choices

This is where strategy happens. Choices are:

```
Where to play:
  - Which user segments?
  - Which geographies?
  - Which use cases?
  - Which platform (mobile, web, API)?

How to win:
  - What is our unique value proposition?
  - What trade-offs are we willing to make?
  - What are we explicitly NOT doing?
```

**The "Not Doing" list is the strategy.** If your strategy doesn't have a "Not Doing" list, it's not a strategy — it's a wish list.

> *"The quality of a strategy is measured by what it excludes, not what it includes."*

**Real example from Headspace**: When Janakiraman was CPO, Headspace had to choose: be a meditation app or a mental health platform. The strategy chose mental health platform — which meant *not* optimizing for daily meditation streaks (their core metric at the time) and instead investing in therapy, coaching, and clinical tools.

---

### Step 4: Build the Roadmap from the Choices

Only now — after questions, landscape, and choices — do you build a roadmap. The roadmap is the *execution plan* for the strategy, not the strategy itself.

**The roadmap should answer**: "Given our choices, what do we build first, second, and third — and why in that order?"

| Roadmap Priority Criteria | Question |
|--------------------------|----------|
| **Strategic alignment** | Does this initiative directly serve our strategic choices? |
| **Impact confidence** | How confident are we this will move the metric? |
| **Sequencing dependencies** | Does anything else need to be built first? |
| **Resource reality** | Can our team actually ship this in the timeline? |

---

### Step 5: Run Strategy Sprints

Janakiraman's most tactical contribution: the **Strategy Sprint** — an 8–12 week focused cycle for solving a specific strategic question.

```
Week 1-2: Frame the question + landscape research
Week 3-4: Generate hypotheses (3-5 strategic options)
Week 5-6: Stress-test each option (data, user research, feasibility)
Week 7-8: Make the choice + socialize with stakeholders
Week 9-12: Execute the first iteration + measure
```

The sprint format prevents the most common strategy failure: **analysis paralysis**. By time-boxing the process, the team is forced to make a decision with imperfect information — which is always the case anyway.

## 📊 The Strategy Quality Test

Janakiraman offers a diagnostic for existing strategies:

| Test | Passing | Failing |
|------|---------|---------|
| **The "Not Doing" test** | Strategy explicitly excludes options | Strategy includes everything |
| **The 2-minute test** | Anyone on the team can explain the strategy in 2 minutes | Only the PM or CPO can explain it |
| **The trade-off test** | Strategy requires genuine trade-offs | Strategy avoids hard choices |
| **The decision filter test** | Strategy helps the team make daily decisions without a meeting | Strategy is too abstract to guide real work |
| **The competitor test** | A competitor would *not* choose the same strategy | Strategy is generic industry best practice |

> *"If your competitor could copy your strategy word-for-word and it would still make sense for them — you don't have a strategy. You have a goal."*

## 🔍 AI and the Future of Strategy

Janakiraman addresses how AI changes product strategy work:

**What AI can do for strategy:**
- Synthesize competitive landscapes faster
- Model scenario outcomes
- Generate hypothesis options
- Stress-test assumptions against data

**What AI cannot do for strategy:**
- Make the choice (that requires judgment + conviction)
- Understand organizational politics and constraints
- Factor in team morale and culture
- Decide what *not* to do (AI optimizes; strategy sacrifices)

> *"AI will make the analysis phase of strategy 10x faster. It won't make the choice phase any easier. That's still the PM's job — and it's getting harder, not easier."*

## ✅ Action Items for PMs This Week

- [ ] **Write your strategic question**: In one sentence, what is the choice your product team is currently facing? If you can't write it, schedule time to figure it out.
- [ ] **Build your "Not Doing" list**: Write 3-5 things your product is explicitly *not* doing this quarter. Share it with your team. If they're surprised, your strategy wasn't clear.
- [ ] **Run the 2-minute test**: Ask 3 team members to explain your product strategy in 2 minutes. Record the answers. The variance tells you how aligned your team really is.
- [ ] **Schedule a strategy sprint**: Pick one unresolved strategic question and time-box 8 weeks to answer it. Use Janakiraman's sprint format.
- [ ] **Map small-s vs. Big-S**: Which of your current initiatives are small-s (solving today's problem) and which are Big-S (defining tomorrow's identity)? If the Big-S column is empty, that's your biggest risk.
